Resistance values are usually known only up to some tolerance guaran- teed by the manufacturer of the resistor. For example, if you buy a resis- tor labeled “6.8 ohms with 10% tolerance” you can only be sure that the resistor has a resistance between 6.8 - 0.68 = 6.12 and 6.8+0.68 = 7.48 ohms. us, if you have a 6.8-ohm 10% resistor in parallel with a 4.7-ohm 5% resistor, the resistance of the combination can range from about 2.58 ohms (if the two resistors are at the lower bounds) to about 2.97 ohms (if the two resistors are at the upper bounds). Alyssa’s idea is to implement “interval arithmetic” as a set of arith- metic operations for combining “intervals” (objects that represent the range of possible values of an inexact quantity). e result of adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing two intervals is itself an interval, representing the range of the result. Alyssa postulates the existence of an abstract object called an “in- terval” that has two endpoints: a lower bound and an upper bound. She also presumes that, given the endpoints of an interval, she can con- struct the interval using the data constructor make-interval. Alyssa ﬁrst writes a procedure for adding two intervals.
